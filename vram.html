VRAM

The VRAM (Video Random Access Memory) of a GPU (Graphics Processing Unit) refers to the dedicated memory that is specifically designed for storing and accessing graphical data. It is a type of RAM that provides high-speed data access for the GPU, enabling it to handle and process large amounts of graphics-related information efficiently.

VRAM is used to store textures, shaders, framebuffers, and other data necessary for rendering images and videos on the screen. The GPU accesses the VRAM directly, allowing for faster data transfer compared to accessing system RAM.

The amount of VRAM in a GPU is an important specification that determines the GPU's capability to handle complex graphics tasks and higher-resolution displays. GPUs with higher VRAM capacities can store more data, which is particularly beneficial for tasks such as gaming, 3D modeling, video editing, and other graphically demanding applications.

VRAM capacities vary across different GPUs and models, typically ranging from 2GB to 24GB or more in high-end professional GPUs. The specific VRAM capacity required depends on the intended use, display resolution, and the complexity of the graphics being rendered. For casual gaming and general use, GPUs with 6GB to 10GB of VRAM are often sufficient, while more demanding applications may benefit from GPUs with higher VRAM capacities.

But lately this has changed due to triple A titles being more demanding, GPUs with lower than 16GB of VRAM are falling behind. That's why we recommend if you are going to spend more than $350 on a GPU don't buy anything with less than 16GB of VRAM
