How does a power supply work?

As you know, the power supply is an essential component of a computer as it supplies power and does so correctly. Not only does it convert alternating current into direct current, but it also adjusts the voltages to be suitable in each case.

In addition to that, modern power supplies also incorporate active and passive protection technologies. They can receive signals from the operating system through the motherboard to shut down, or they can be powered on by pressing the power button, which sends a 5V signal (the PSU has a circuit that supplies 5V called VSB or standby voltage, allowing the button to function even when the system is off) to the power supply unit indicating that it should turn on.

A PC power supply is vital in a computer system that supplies electrical power to all other components. It is responsible for converting the electric current from the wall outlet into a suitable and stable form for use by the computer. The power supply provides different voltages (such as +12V, +5V, and +3.3V) and currents required to power the motherboard, processor, graphics card, hard drives, fans, and other connected devices.

Some components, such as the CPU, GPU, RAM, etc., may operate at different voltages than those supplied by the power supply. In such cases, additional voltage regulation modules are used to convert to the correct voltage. For example, modern CPUs typically operate at voltages around 1V. For more information, you can read this other article.

In addition to providing necessary power, a PC power supply also plays an important role in regulating and protecting the system. This involves maintaining a stable and safe power supply to prevent voltage fluctuations that could damage internal computer components. It also includes features for protection against overvoltage, short circuits, and over-temperature to safeguard the power supply and other system components.

PC power supplies typically have a rated power expressed in watts (W), indicating the maximum amount of power they can continuously supply. Choosing an appropriate power supply involves considering the power requirements of the computer components, as well as energy efficiency, connectors, and any additional features needed.
